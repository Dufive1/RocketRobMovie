# "The Silence"

## The Power Goes Offline — Screenplay Format

**Runtime**: ~8 minutes
**Music**: Quiet. Thinning. Then nothing. The score itself should feel like something is being withdrawn.
**Position**: Mid-Act Two (~40:00–48:00). Follows the rising action of Rob's double life. Precedes the hockey game.

---

## PART ONE: THE TRIGGER

### INT. MAX'S BEDROOM — NIGHT

Late. The hallway light is on. Max's hockey gear is scattered across the floor. His Rocket Rob drawings cover the wall above his bed. The dish-towel cape hangs on his bedpost.

Max is in bed. Eyes open. Staring at the ceiling.

He's been waiting.

The front door opens. Footsteps. A pause. Then Rob appears in the doorway. Tie loosened. Exhausted. A scratch on his hand he's trying to hide.

                              ROB
                         (whispering)
                    Hey, bud. You still up?

                              MAX
                    You missed the story.

Beat. Rob steps in. Sits on the edge of the bed.

                              ROB
                    I know. I'm sorry, bud. Tomorrow I'll—

                              MAX
                    You said that yesterday.

Not angry. Not accusing. Just... factual. A kid stating what's true.

Rob doesn't have an answer. He reaches to ruffle Max's hair.

Max rolls over. Pulls the blanket up. Goes still.

                              ROB
                    Max—

Nothing.

Rob sits there. The room is quiet. On the wall above Max's bed, Rocket Rob flies through a dozen crayon cities. The hero Max invented. The hero who always came home.

Rob stands. Walks to the door. Looks back.

No narration. No sleepy whisper of "Rocket Rob completes his secret mission." No dramatic voice-over. Just a dad in a doorway and a kid who's done waiting.

Rob pulls the door mostly closed.

HOLD on the sliver of hallway light across Max's bed.

---

## PART TWO: THE FADE

### INT. LAVOIE KITCHEN — MORNING (NEXT DAY)

Rob is at the counter making breakfast. Pancakes. He flips one — it goes sideways, lands half off the plate.

Max walks in. Hockey bag over his shoulder. He glances at the mess.

No narration.

In Act One, this moment would have been: Max whispering, "The hero fuels up for his next mission!" and the pancake flip becoming an epic citizen rescue in the imagination overlay.

Now it's just a man making a bad pancake. And a kid getting cereal instead.

                              ROB
                    Pancakes?

                              MAX
                    I'm good.

Max sits down. Eats. Doesn't look up.

Rob stands at the counter. The pancake sits on the plate, half off, cooling.

### EXT. LAVOIE DRIVEWAY — MORNING (CONTINUOUS)

Rob drives Max to school. In Act One, this was a "rocket launch" — Max narrating the car blasting through the atmosphere, the street becoming a runway, the school becoming mission HQ.

Now it's a car pulling out of a driveway. Traffic. A red light. Radio playing quietly.

Max stares out the window.

                              ROB
                    Big practice today, right?

                              MAX
                    Yeah.

Silence. Rob drums his fingers on the steering wheel. Searches for something to say. Doesn't find it.

They arrive at school. Max grabs his bag.

                              MAX
                    Bye.

He's gone before Rob can respond.

Rob watches him walk in. Small. Backpack too big. No backward glance.

### INT. MAX'S BEDROOM — AFTERNOON

Max comes home. Drops his bag.

He opens his desk drawer. Picks up his Rocket Rob action figures — the ones he and Rob used to stage elaborate battles with on the living room floor.

He looks at them for a moment.

Then puts them in the bottom drawer. Under some books.

CLOSE ON: the dish-towel cape, still draped on his chair. Max looks at it.

He folds it. Neatly. Sets it on the chair seat.

Sits at his desk. Opens a school textbook.

The cape stays folded.

---

## PART THREE: THE ABSENCE

*The following scenes mirror earlier moments from Act One — the same shots, the same framing, the same situations. But without the narration. Without the imagination overlay. The contrast should feel like someone turned the color down on a television. Not literally — the palette stays warm — but emotionally. Something essential has been withdrawn.*

### INT. LAVOIE KITCHEN — EVENING

The sink is dripping. Again.

Rob opens the cabinet under the sink. Gets on his back. Reaches for the wrench.

In Act One, this was the Hydro Blaster. Max crouched in the doorway whispering combat narration. The water spray was enemy fire. The wrench was a weapon.

Now it's a leaking pipe. Rob turns the wrench. Water sprays in his face. He sighs. Wipes his eyes.

No Max in the doorway. No narration. No imagination overlay.

Just a man fixing a sink.

### INT. TITAN SPORTS TECH — DAY

Rob at his desk. Team Canada mug. Max's drawings pinned to the wall.

He stares at his screen. An alert comes in — another grid anomaly. Watt's machine probing the city's infrastructure.

In Act One, Rob would have straightened up, jaw set, eyes bright. The dad-logic hero seeing the mission clearly. Max's voice in his head: "Rocket Rob detects a disturbance!"

Now Rob stares at the screen. Rubs his eyes. His hand hovers over his phone — should he call it in? Handle it? Or just... go home on time for once?

He hesitates. For the first time in the film, the hero hesitates.

He picks up the phone. Puts it down. Picks it up again.

A soft chirp from Rob's bag. The cat sidekick pokes his head out, the tiny screen on his harness blinking:
**[ANOMALOUS ENERGY — SECTOR 4]**

                              ROB
                    Not now.

He closes his laptop. Sits in the silence of his cubicle.

HOLD on Max's drawings pinned to the wall behind him. Rocket Rob in every one. Flying. Saving. Smiling. A record of belief that's gone quiet.

### EXT. CITY STREET — EVENING

Rob walks to his car. The city around him is ordinary. Streetlights. Traffic noise. A bus hissing at a stop.

In Act One, this walk would have had scale — the buildings towering heroically, the street a canyon, Rob's stride carrying purpose.

Now: a man in a gray suit walking to his car in a parking lot. Shoulders slightly lower than before.

He reaches his car. Fumbles the keys. Drops them. Bends down to pick them up.

No one is watching. No one is narrating.

---

## PART FOUR: ROB'S DIMINISHMENT

### EXT. CITY — NIGHT

Another grid failure. Power out on the east side. Watt is probing again.

Rob is on the rooftop in the suit. Cape. Mask. Belt buckle.

But something is different. He stands at the edge and looks down. In Act One, he would have jumped without thinking. Max's belief propelling him forward. The voice in his head telling him he could fly.

Now he stands at the edge. Looks down. The distance is real.

He jumps — but the landing is harder than it used to be. He stumbles. Catches himself against a wall.

He runs across rooftops. But he's slower. Less sure.

He reaches the grid relay and starts working on it. His hands are steady, but his problem-solving is off. He tries the wrong panel first. Backtracks. Loses time.

The cat pads silently across the rooftop and sits beside him. The harness screen blinks:
**[-14% REACTION TIME]**

                              ROB
                    I'm fine.

The screen blinks again:
**[ERROR RATE: ↑]**

                              ROB
                    I said I'm FINE.

He fixes the relay. The lights come back on.

Rob sits on the rooftop edge. Looks at the city. Looks toward his neighborhood. Toward the house where Max is sleeping.

He reaches for his belt buckle. Out of habit, he flips it open — the drawing is still there. "My Dad Saves The World."

He stares at it for a long time.

The drawing was put there when Max still believed. The drawing hasn't changed. But the voice that gave it power has gone quiet.

Rob closes the buckle. Stands up. Goes home.

### Director's Notes

**The Power Source**: This sequence is not a montage of sadness. It's a structural event. The film's power source — Max's belief — has gone offline, and the audience should feel it as a *withdrawal*, not just a mood shift. Scenes should be shot identically to their Act One counterparts (same angles, same framing, same lighting setup) but without the imagination overlay and without the narration. The absence creates the emotion, not the acting.

**Rob's Physical Change**: Rob should be measurably less effective in every dimension. His hero work is sloppier. His dad work is more distant. His confidence is eroded. This is not depression — it's the absence of the thing that made him brave. A dad without his kid's belief is just a tired man in a suit. The harness readout quantifies the diminishment for kids who might not feel the subtlety — and the cat's wordless presence beside Rob on the rooftop makes it land harder than any spoken line could.

**The Mirrored Scenes**: The key to The Silence is repetition-with-absence. Every audience member (kid or adult) will recognize the sink scene, the car scene, the office scene from Act One. When they play out again without the magic, the audience supplies the memory of what's missing. They narrate the absence themselves. That's more devastating than showing it.

**No Music Cue**: The score should thin across these scenes. By the time Rob is on the rooftop, there should be almost nothing — just ambient city noise. The audience should feel the sonic emptiness the same way they feel the narrative emptiness. The music returns for the hockey game, which follows immediately.

**Connection to What Follows**: The Silence sets up the hockey game with maximum emotional stakes. When Rob misses the championship, the audience isn't just sad for Max — they're watching a hero who's lost his power source fail to show up for the person who gave it to him. And when Max says "It's okay... my dad saves bigger things," it's devastating because Max is *still defending Rob* even after the belief went quiet. He stopped narrating. He didn't stop loving.

**The Drawing in the Buckle**: Rob looking at Max's drawing on the rooftop plants the detail that pays off in the final battle. The audience now knows it's there. When Rob finds it again during the climax, the recognition is instant. But the emotional weight is different — the first time he looks at it, it's a reminder of what he's lost. The second time, it's the thing that tells him what heroism actually means.
