# "What Makes a Hero"

## Final Battle and Resolution — Screenplay Format

**Runtime**: ~8 minutes
**Music**: Dramatic orchestral to quiet piano resolution

---

## PART ONE: THE STORM

### EXT. CITY SKYLINE — NIGHT

The worst storm the city has ever seen. Not natural — the clouds pulse with electric blue energy. VICTOR WATT's machine, now at full power, sits atop the Titan Sports Tech tower, cables running into the city grid like veins.

Lightning TEARS across the skyline.

Below: chaos. Traffic lights dead. Hospital backup generators kicking in. Emergency sirens.

### EXT. CITY STREETS — CONTINUOUS

CLAIRE — no, VELA — moves through the streets below. Sleek suit. No cape. She's fast. Faster than fast. She's done this before.

She directs civilians to shelter. Reroutes a downed power line with a move that looks practiced. Shouts coordinates to emergency crews like someone who's run an evacuation before.

A transformer EXPLODES nearby. Claire shields a family with her body. The energy dissipates around her — a faint blue shimmer.

She looks up at the Titan tower. The rooftop. She knows Rob is heading there.

                              CLAIRE
                         (into a comm device)
                    The ground is covered. Go.

### EXT. TITAN SPORTS TECH TOWER — ROOFTOP — CONTINUOUS

ROCKET ROB lands on the roof. Hard landing. He's been fighting through Watt's defenses to get here.

His cape is ripped. His mask is cracked at the edge. The RR belt buckle is scratched but intact.

He's exhausted. But he's here. And for the first time, he's not alone.

VICTOR WATT stands beside his machine. Calm. Almost disappointed.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    You know, I really thought you'd
                    be smarter than this.

                              ROB
                    I get that a lot.

Watt gestures at the machine. It HUMS with power. The cables pulse.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    This machine will provide unlimited
                    energy to this entire city. Everything
                    they need. Forever.

                              ROB
                    And what's the cost?

                              VICTOR WATT
                    Control. Mine.

                              ROB
                    That's a big cost.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    That's what heroes do, Rob. They make
                    the choices no one else will.

Watt steps closer. His voice drops. Personal now.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    You could have been great.
                    Instead you chose bedtime stories
                    and hockey games.

Rob doesn't answer immediately. The wind whips his torn cape.

                              ROB
                    Those are great.

Watt's expression hardens.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    Heroes don't tuck kids into bed.

---

## PART TWO: THE CHOICE

Watt FIRES. A massive bolt of electricity.

Rob dodges. Barely. The blast carves a trench in the rooftop behind him.

They fight. It's not elegant — Rob is outmatched in raw power. Watt is stronger, more advanced, more rested. Rob takes hits. Gets back up. Takes more.

Watt pins Rob against a railing. The city stretches below them, going dark block by block as the machine drains more power.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    Look at them. They need someone
                    who's willing to sacrifice everything.

                              ROB
                         (strained)
                    You already did that. How'd it
                    work out?

Watt's grip tightens. But something flickers in his eyes. Just for a moment.

He throws Rob aside.

Rob slides across the rooftop. Stops near the machine.

He looks at it. The cables. The power readings. He could destroy it — one big hit, one dramatic hero moment. But the surge would black out the city for hours. Hospitals. Traffic. People.

Or he could shut it down carefully. Absorb the energy overload himself. Stabilize the grid.

No dramatic explosion. No hero moment. Just... the right choice.

Rob reaches for the machine's core.

### CLOSE ON: Rob's hand

His glove is torn. Underneath — his wedding ring.

### CLOSE ON: Rob's belt buckle

Scratched. Dented. But through the damage, something is visible — tucked behind the buckle, a tiny folded piece of paper.

Rob pulls it out.

INSERT — MAX'S DRAWING:

Rocket Rob flying through the sky with a hockey stick. Stars around him. The city safe below.

Written at the bottom in wobbly handwriting:

**"My Dad Saves The World."**

Rob stares at it.

And he understands.

Max never believed in Rocket Rob because he saved the city. Max believed in him because he showed up.

Rob folds the drawing carefully. Puts it back.

He turns to Watt.

                              ROB
                    You're right about one thing.
                    Heroes make the choices no one
                    else will.

Rob grabs the machine's core. Energy SURGES into him. It's agonizing. His suit crackles. His cape shreds.

                              VICTOR WATT
                    What are you DOING? You'll overload
                    the entire—

                              ROB
                    I'm not overloading it. I'm
                    absorbing it.

The machine SCREAMS. Energy pours through Rob. The city grid FLICKERS — and then STABILIZES. Block by block, lights return. Steady. Safe.

The machine dies. Collapses into scrap.

Rob falls to his knees. Smoking. Barely conscious.

Watt stands in the sudden silence. His machine — his power, his purpose — is gone.

                              VICTOR WATT
                         (quiet, almost broken)
                    ...Why?

Rob looks up at him. Exhausted. Barely able to speak.

                              ROB
                    Because someone's waiting for me
                    at home.

Watt stares at him. For the first time, the mask slips. Not anger. Not contempt.

Loneliness.

Watt turns. Walks to the edge of the roof.

                              VICTOR WATT
                         (not turning around)
                    You were always the better one of us.

He pauses at the edge. Reaches into his coat. Pulls out something small — a photograph, creased and soft from years of carrying. We don't see what's in it. He looks at it for a long moment. Something passes across his face that isn't anger or contempt. Something older.

He puts it back. Carefully.

Then steps off the edge and disappears into the dark.

### EXT. CITY STREETS — CONTINUOUS

Below, Claire sees a shadow fall past the tower. She looks up. Nothing.

She touches her comm.

                              CLAIRE
                    Rob?

### EXT. TITAN SPORTS TECH TOWER — ROOFTOP — CONTINUOUS

Rob stays on his knees for a moment. Breathing.

                              ROB
                         (into comm, quiet)
                    I'm here.

Beat.

                              CLAIRE
                    Come home.

Rob stands. Looks toward the city. Toward the arena. Toward home.

---

## PART THREE: THE RESOLUTION

### INT. LAVOIE HOME — MAX'S BEDROOM — NIGHT

Late. The house is quiet.

Max is in bed. Asleep. His hockey stick leans against the wall. His gear is scattered on the floor. A drawing of Rocket Rob is pinned above his bed.

The bedroom door opens. A sliver of hallway light.

Rob leans in the doorway. No suit. No cape. Just Rob in a t-shirt and jeans. A bandage on his hand. Tired eyes.

He watches Max sleep. Just for a moment.

He crosses to the bed. Sits on the edge.

Max stirs. Opens his eyes halfway.

                              MAX
                         (sleepy)
                    Dad?

                              ROB
                    Hey, bud. Go back to sleep.

                              MAX
                    Did you save the world?

Rob looks at him. At the drawings on the wall. At the hockey stick. At this kid who still believes.

                              ROB
                    Something like that.

Max smiles. Eyes closing.

                              MAX
                    I knew it.

He's asleep again.

Rob pulls the blanket up. Stays sitting for a moment longer than he needs to.

### INT. LAVOIE KITCHEN — CONTINUOUS

Rob walks to the kitchen. Opens the fridge. The light illuminates his face — and the dozens of drawings covering the fridge door. Rocket Rob in every one. Flying. Saving. Smiling.

He takes out a glass of water. Leans against the counter.

CLAIRE appears in the doorway. Robe. Hair down. She takes one look at him and knows.

                              CLAIRE
                    Still saving the world?

Rob looks toward Max's room. The hallway is dark and warm.

He takes a sip of water. Sets it down.

                              ROB
                    Just the parts that matter.

Claire crosses to him. Leans against his shoulder.

They stand in the kitchen. Quiet. The fridge hums. Max's drawings surround them.

---

### EXT. LAVOIE HOME — CONTINUOUS

PULL BACK through the kitchen window. The warm light inside. Two figures standing close.

WIDER: the house on its street. Porch light on. Hockey net in the driveway.

WIDER: the neighborhood. The city beyond. Lights steady. Safe.

WIDER: the skyline. The bridge. The Titan tower dark now. Stars above.

And then — Max's voice.

For the first time since the Silence began. The narration that disappeared mid-film. The voice that gave this movie its soul and then went quiet.

It's back. But different.

                              MAX (V.O.)
                         (soft, sleepy, certain)
                    Every city has a hero.

Not dramatic. Not excited. Not playing pretend.

                              MAX (V.O.)
                    Ours has the best one.

This isn't a kid narrating a superhero movie anymore. This is a child who outgrew the fantasy, sat in the silence, and chose to believe anyway.

HOLD on the city. Quiet. Safe. Ordinary.

TITLE CARD:

# ROCKET ROB

FADE TO BLACK.

---

### Director's Notes

**Claire in the Battle**: Claire is Vela throughout Act Three. She doesn't take over Rob's fight — she handles the ground while he handles the sky. They're a team for the first time. The "Come home" line on the comm is the emotional resolution of her arc: she's no longer carrying everything alone, and she's letting Rob know she's there.

**Watt's Exit**: Victor Watt doesn't get arrested or destroyed. He walks away, broken, alone. The photo he carries — unseen by the audience — tells us everything: he still has something personal he never threw away. He could have been Rob. His loneliness is his punishment. This leaves the door open for a redemption arc.

**The Drawing in the Belt Buckle**: This detail must be established earlier (in the montage or a quiet scene) so that when Rob finds it during the battle, the audience already knows what it is. The power of the moment comes from recognition, not surprise.

**"Just the parts that matter"**: This is the thesis statement of the entire film, delivered in the quietest possible way — a tired dad in a kitchen. Not a speech. Not a declaration. Just truth.

**The Pull-Back**: The final shot should feel like the audience is being gently let go. The warmth of the kitchen, the house, the street, the city, the sky — each wider shot carries the same feeling: everything is okay. Not perfect. Not dramatic. Just okay. And that's enough.

**Max's Returned Voice**: This is the Pixar moment. Max's narration disappeared mid-film when he outgrew the Rocket Rob fantasy. The Silence lasted roughly 30 minutes of screen time. The audience has been living without it. When it returns — soft, sleepy, changed — the relief and emotion should be overwhelming. The words are almost the same as the opening. But they mean something completely different. A choice is more powerful than faith.
