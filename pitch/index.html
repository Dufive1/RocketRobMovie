Rocket Rob

"Can an ordinary dad still be a hero?"

Animated Feature Film
Audience

The Heart
"Being a hero isn't about saving the world. It's about being there for the people who believe in you."
The underneath truth: belief is the superpower — and it was always Max's.

Rob didn't become a hero and then earn his son's belief. Max's belief is what made Rob a hero. When that belief goes silent mid-film, the power goes with it. When it returns — not as faith, but as a choice — it's more powerful than it ever was.
The Family

Characters

Every character represents a different belief about what it means to be a hero. When they clash, the story becomes emotional and compelling.

Rob Lavoie / Rocket Rob

The Reluctant Hero

A Canadian dad working at Titan Sports Tech, trying to balance career demands with being the father Max deserves. Capable, optimistic, a little goofy, and always slightly tired. He solves every problem with dad logic.

Claire Lavoie / Vela

The Grounding Force — With Her Own Arc

A former superhero who chose family over the cape. She's been carrying the weight of that choice — and the invisible load of everything Rob doesn't see — alone for years. Her mid-film reveal as Vela recontextualizes everything. B-theme: "Being strong doesn't mean doing it alone."

Max Lavoie

The Believer — Tested by Silence

The emotional core of the film. Hockey-obsessed, wildly imaginative, and completely certain his dad is the greatest hero ever. Max narrates Rob's life like a superhero movie — until mid-film, when he outgrows the fantasy. The narration goes silent. When it returns at the end, it's no longer pretend. It's a choice.

Victor Watt

The Dark Mirror — The False Answer

Rob's former colleague and philosophical antagonist. Both Canadian, both ambitious — but Watt chose power over family. Now rich, powerful, and alone. He needs Rob to fail to prove his sacrifice was worth it. His exit — a creased photo he never threw away — is the Pixar gut punch.

Maple — The Sidekick

Comic Relief with Accidental Truth

An orange tabby who adopted Rob long before Rob noticed. Wears a repurposed Titan tech harness and a self-applied RR badge that's slightly crooked. Completely literal, utterly loyal, and always underfoot at the worst possible moment. Even the cat slowly learns the theme.

The Story

Story Spine

The Pixar Story Spine — one emotional chain of events that powers the entire film.

The world of Rocket Rob at first light. A city where the line between ordinary and extraordinary is as thin as a child's belief — and a red streak in the morning sky that might be nothing. Or might be everything.
Once upon a time
Rob Lavoie was an ordinary dad who worked a demanding job at Titan Sports Tech, rushing between meetings, emails, and deadlines while trying his best to be present for his son Max. But in Max's eyes, Rob wasn't ordinary. He was Rocket Rob — the greatest superhero in the world.
Every day
Rob tried to balance work, family, and life. Max turned everything Rob did into a superhero adventure — fixing the sink became stopping a flood monster, driving to hockey practice became a rocket launch. Rob laughed along, but deep down he worried he was failing at both work and fatherhood.
One day
Victor Watt — Rob's former colleague, now powerful and alone — threatens the city with technology Rob helped build. But the real danger isn't the machine. It's Watt's question: Can you save the world and still make it home for bedtime? Rob doesn't have an answer. But Max is watching — and in Max's version, Dad always finds one.
Because of that
Rob tries to be both — the hero the city needs and the dad Max believes in. But every save costs a bedtime story. Every victory means an empty chair at dinner. Max's narration gets quieter — then stops entirely. The imagination track goes silent. Claire absorbs everything Rob can't do. The invisible load cracks. Watt keeps repeating: "Not both."
Until finally
Claire reveals she was Vela — a hero who chose family over the cape. Rob realizes he's about to make the opposite choice. In the final storm, he finds Max's crumpled drawing behind his belt buckle — "My Dad Saves The World." He doesn't win with power. He chooses the quiet right thing over the dramatic hero moment. And Watt, reaching for a photo he never threw away, understands too late what he gave up.
Ever since then
Rob still has a normal job. Claire doesn't carry the weight alone anymore. And Max's voice returns one last time — soft, sleepy, certain. Not a kid playing pretend. A child who outgrew the fantasy, sat in the silence, and chose to believe anyway.

The Opposition

Villain Lineup

Pixar villains are emotionally relatable. Victor Watt drives the film. The remaining villains expand the world in the companion series.

Victor Watt

Film — Main Antagonist
"Heroes don't tuck kids into bed. They change the world."

Rob's former colleague and dark mirror. Both Canadian, both ambitious — but Watt chose power over family. Now rich, powerful, and alone. He needs Rob to fail to prove his sacrifice was worth it. His exit — a creased photo he never threw away — is the Pixar gut punch.

The Countdown

Series Expansion
"Your time is UP."

A comedic villain who literally manifests from emails, notifications, and buzzing phones. Creates chaos that keeps Rob from getting home. Kids laugh. Parents feel personally attacked.

Dr. Overtime

Series Expansion
"There's always more work to be done."

A time-obsessed villain with clock-based powers. Manipulates time to trap Rob in endless work loops. Represents the feeling that there's never enough time.

Coach Crusher

Series Expansion
"Second place is first loser."

A rival youth hockey coach obsessed with winning at all costs. No superpowers — just the "win at all costs" philosophy that corrupts youth sports. Rob must teach Max that winning isn't everything.

The Homework Hydra

Series Expansion
"You solved math? Here's MORE math."

A multi-headed monster — each head is a school subject. Every time Rob solves a problem, two more appear. The nightly homework battle made absurd and epic.

The Shadow Dad

Film — Psychological Beat (Act Two)
"They'll understand when they're older."

Rob if he chose work over family. Calm. Powerful. Empty. Exists in Rob's mind during a crisis of confidence. The most Pixar villain in the lineup — think the Bing Bong moment from Inside Out.

Film Structure

Three-Act Breakdown

~90 minutes. Two parallel arcs colliding at the climax.

Act One: "The Greatest Hero Ever" (~25 min)
Max's voice over an epic battle — hard cut to Rob fixing the kitchen sink while Max whispers hero commentary from behind the couch. We meet the Lavoies: a family running on love, hockey schedules, and not enough hours. Claire moves a little too fast, knows a little too much — seeds the audience won't notice yet. The "Little Hero Moments" montage sets the emotional baseline. Then Watt resurfaces with his question, the city shifts, and Rob — for the first time — chooses to act like the hero Max already believes he is.
Act Two: "The Cost" (~45 min)
Every save costs a bedtime story. Every victory, an empty chair. Max's narration gets quieter — then stops entirely. The Silence. Claire's invisible load becomes visible: she's been holding the family together alone, and nobody noticed. The missed championship game. The Shadow Dad — Rob's vision of himself if he chose work and lost everything. Then Claire breaks it open: "I was Vela before you were Rocket Rob." Everything recontextualizes. Rob doesn't hit bottom from a punch. He hits it from understanding what he almost became.
Act Three: "What Makes a Hero" (~20 min)
Final storm. Claire coordinates the ground, Rob takes the sky. He opens the battered belt buckle and finds Max's crumpled drawing — "My Dad Saves The World." He doesn't win with power. He chooses the quiet right thing over the dramatic hero moment. Watt reaches for a photo he never threw away. The hug at the arena. "Just the parts that matter." Max's voice returns — soft, certain, changed.
Post-Film

Series Expansion

Episode concepts that expand the Rocket Rob world beyond the feature film. Max's narration is restored, Claire operates openly as Vela, and the family faces new challenges together.

1

The Greatest Hero Ever

Rob is late to pick up Max from practice — again. He promises to make the championship game. Meanwhile, Victor Watt announces his "revolutionary" energy tech, and Rob pushes back.

2

The Missing Hockey Stick

Max's lucky stick vanishes before a big game. Rocket Rob's quest through the school lost-and-found labyrinth teaches Max that confidence comes from inside.

3

Laundry Day Apocalypse

Claire's away. Rob's in charge. The washing machine becomes a portal. Socks become sentient. Rob gains new respect for the invisible work that holds a family together.

4

The Science Fair Disaster

Max's volcano won't work. The Homework Hydra attacks. Rob and Max combine dad logic with kid imagination to learn that trying together beats winning alone.

5

The Homework Monster

Max can't focus. Rob can't help without doing it for him. The Hydra returns — each head must be defeated differently. Rob learns to let Max struggle and grow.

6

Dad vs. Bedtime

Max refuses to sleep. Every delay opens a mini-adventure. The glass of water becomes a lava-floor quest. Even bedtime is a kind of heroism.

7

Coach Crusher

Max faces Crusher's hyper-competitive team. The "win at all costs" philosophy starts infecting Max. Coach Hank's wisdom shines as Rob teaches Max what really matters.

8

The Countdown

Rob's impossible work deadline manifests as a literal villain — emails and notifications forming a chaos monster. The only way to beat it is to choose to leave.

9

Little Hero Moments

Max finds old home videos. The quiet episode. The Up-style montage. Time is the real villain, and the only way to fight it is to be present right now. This is the one that makes parents cry.

10

What Makes a Hero

Season finale. Max's championship game collides with Watt's full attack on the city. Rob must answer the question the entire series has been asking: what makes a hero?

Proportions

Do: Stylized Pixar proportions. 7-head ratio for adults. Oversized heads. Expressive hands. Athletic but not bulky.

Don't: Realistic anatomy. Comic-book muscle. Adult superhero proportions.

Faces

Do: Large expressive eyes. Thick eyebrows. Rounded features. Clean, readable expressions at any size.

Don't: Small eyes. Sharp angular features. Gritty or realistic detail.

Environments

Do: Rounded architecture. Soft lighting. Bright accents. The "Pixar + Apple" aesthetic — clean, warm, cinematic.

Don't: Harsh angles. Dark desaturated palettes. Photorealistic textures.

Tone

Do: Optimistic even in conflict. Rich night palettes. Warm color temperature. A world kids want to live in.

Don't: Grim. Cynical. Muddy or gray-washed. Scary for young audiences.

Signature Moments

Key Scenes

Six scenes that define the emotional architecture of the film. Each works on two levels — adventure for kids, real feeling for parents.

The world of Rocket Rob: a lived-in home, a kid who believes, a dad who's trying, and a mom who sees everything. This is the family the audience will spend 90 minutes with.

The Greatest Hero Ever

Opening Sequence

Epic superhero battle narrated by Max — then a hard cut to Rob fixing the kitchen sink while Max whispers hero commentary. The audience doesn't know what's real. That's the hook.

"Did the city survive?" — Claire
"Barely." — Rob

Little Hero Moments

Silent Montage

The Up-style montage. No dialogue. Piano and guitar. Tiny Max in a dish-towel cape. First time on skates. Late nights at the office. Drawings accumulating on the fridge. Time passing. Belief staying the same.

"Every hero needs a sidekick." — Max's drawing

The Silence

The Disappearance

Mid-film, Max stops narrating. No announcement. The action figures go in a drawer. The dish-towel cape stays folded. The movie's magic track goes silent. Kids feel something is wrong. Parents feel grief. This is the emotional equivalent of the color draining from the world.

"You missed the story." — Max
"I know. I'm sorry, bud. Tomorrow—"
"You said that yesterday."

The Hockey Game

Emotional Climax

Rob misses Max's championship to save the city. Max sits on the bench watching other dads cheer. "It's okay… my dad saves bigger things." Rob arrives late. Bruised. Max just hugs him. No dialogue needed.

"Did you win?" — Rob
(Max shrugs. Hugs him.)

The Vela Reveal

Second-Act Turning Point

Claire moves like someone who's done this before. Rob sees it. "Claire... how did you—" She drops the mask she's been wearing for years. "I was Vela before you were Rocket Rob." Everything recontextualizes. For kids: the mom is a superhero too. For parents: she gave it up, and nobody noticed.

"Why did you stop?"
"Because I chose something harder."

What Makes a Hero

Final Battle + Resolution

Claire coordinates the ground. Rob takes the sky. The drawing in the belt buckle. The quiet right choice. Watt reaches for a photo he's carried for years. "You were always the better one of us." Max's voice returns — soft, certain, changed.

"Just the parts that matter." — Rob
Honest Assessment

Why This. Why Now.

We know the territory. Here's why this story belongs in it — and why it's different from anything already there.

The Comparison They'll Make
The Incredibles
  • Superhero family trying to live normally
  • Dad wrestling with hero vs. father identity
  • Wife who was also a hero and walked away
  • Kid who believes in their parent
  • Pixar house style
The surface read is real. Any exec will say it. We say it first.
But
The Actual Difference
Rocket Rob
  • Rob has no powers — he's genuinely ordinary
  • The "superpower" belongs to Max, not Rob
  • The thesis is the opposite of Incredibles
  • The Silence — a child's belief going quiet — is the climax
  • The invisible load, parental burnout, equity — 2026 parents
The Incredibles says: "Extraordinary people shouldn't hide it."
Rocket Rob says: "Ordinary people are already extraordinary — if they show up."
The superpower was never Rob's.
It was always Max's. A child's belief in an imperfect dad is the engine of the entire film. When Max stops believing — the hero disappears. When he chooses to believe again, after the silence, after the missed game, after everything — that's the most heroic act in the story. That's not The Incredibles. That's something new.
Why Now
⏱
The Cultural Moment
The conversation around the invisible domestic load, mental load, and parenting equity has shifted dramatically since 2004. A huge portion of your adult audience is living this film right now. They just haven't seen it on screen yet.
🍁
Cultural Specificity
Pixar films are set in a nowhere-America. Rocket Rob is unapologetically Canadian — the hockey rink, the suburban texture, the particular warmth of a country that doesn't often see itself as the setting for a hero's journey. That specificity creates authenticity.
📺
The Format Opportunity
The "little hero moments" structure is as strong as a series premise as it is a feature. Netflix, Apple TV+, and Disney+ are actively acquiring animated family IP. The short film path — built around The Silence — gets this concept in rooms a spec feature can't reach.
🎬
Canadian Content Funding
Telefilm Canada, the CMF, and provincial arts councils represent a real funding path for animated Canadian content — one where the cultural specificity of this story is an asset, not a risk. The economics work differently here.
