# Max Lavoie — Character Profile

> "Every city has a hero… but OUR city has the greatest hero EVER."

## Real Name

Max Lavoie

## Age

8 years old — but plays younger in Act One, earns older by Act Two.

The film doesn't literally age Max. But repeated absence accelerates a certain kind of growing up. Every missed bedtime is a small piece of childhood that doesn't come back. By The Silence, Max hasn't just gotten older — he's been made older. That's the gut punch.

| Story Moment | Emotional Age | What It Means |
|---|---|---|
| Opening — narrating everything | 7-ish | Pure, effortless imaginative belief. Costs nothing. |
| Act 1 montage | 7–8 | The bond at its most alive. |
| Narration fading | 8 | Starting to feel the weight of waiting. |
| The Silence | 8 going on 9 | Quietly outgrown — not by choice yet, just by accumulation. |
| Hockey game bench | 9 (emotional) | "It's okay… my dad saves bigger things." Too composed for an 8-year-old. He's protecting his dad even after the belief went quiet. |
| Final narration choice | Fully 9 | Old enough to know exactly what he's choosing. That's what gives it weight. |

The animators, voice director, and writers should track this arc across the film. Max in the opening and Max at The Silence should feel like the same kid — but noticeably quieter. Not sadder. Just a little older than he was supposed to be yet.

## Role

The power source. Max doesn't just *feel* the story — he *powers* it. His imagination is the engine. His belief is the superpower. Every time Max whispers hero commentary over a mundane scene, he isn't decorating reality — he's transforming it. His belief is the mechanism by which an ordinary dad becomes Rocket Rob. If the audience believes Max, they believe the movie. And if Max stops believing, the movie — and Rob — lose their power.

## The Core Truth

Max doesn't measure heroism by success, career, or power. He measures it by kindness, effort, and presence. He sees his dad fix a sink and narrates it as stopping a flood monster. He sees his dad drive to hockey practice and narrates it as a rocket launch.

But Max's narration isn't decoration. It's the mechanism by which Rob becomes Rocket Rob. The imagination track IS the power. Every whisper of "Rocket Rob saves the day" is doing something real — giving his dad the courage to try harder, be braver, show up when he'd rather stay in bed. The cape was always a dish towel. The rocket was always a car. But the belief was real, and the belief made everything else real enough to matter.

Max is the power source of the story. Where he points, the audience's heart follows. Where he goes silent, the hero diminishes.

## Personality

| Trait | Expression |
|-------|-----------|
| Huge imagination | Transforms everything into a superhero adventure |
| Hockey-obsessed | Wears his gear constantly, dreams of the big game |
| Believes in his dad completely | Unshakeable faith that Rob is a hero |
| Very expressive | Big reactions, dramatic narration, physical comedy |
| Brave in a kid way | Not fearless — brave because his dad taught him to try |

## Running Gag

Max narrates his dad's life like it's a superhero movie.

> Rob fixing the sink.
> Max, whispering dramatically: "Rocket Rob repairs the Hydro Blaster!"

> Rob making breakfast.
> Max: "The hero fuels up for his next mission!"

## Expression Range

- Awestruck wonder (watching his dad do anything)
- Dramatic narrator (whispering hero commentary)
- Competitive fire (on the hockey ice)
- Quiet vulnerability (sitting on the bench when dad isn't there)
- Pure joy (hugging Rob after the game)

## Appearance

### Face
- Huge excited eyes — sky blue like Rob's (`#3FA9F5`)
- Round face, soft features
- Big smile (his default state)
- Thick eyebrows (family trait — matches Rob)

### Hair
- Similar brown to Rob's but messier, less controlled
- Kid energy — never perfectly styled

### Costume (Everyday)
- Hockey helmet — wears it half the time, even off the ice
- Slightly oversized hockey gear (the gear wears him)
- Canadian-themed hockey jersey — the connection to Rob's heritage
- Small red cape he wears at home (a dish towel Rob tied on him)

### Costume (Hockey)
- Full hockey gear with Canadian maple leaf on the jersey
- Red helmet
- Slightly too-big gloves
- Hockey stick always nearby

## Body Proportions

- Height ratio: 4–5 heads tall
- Even larger head-to-body ratio than adults (Pixar kid standard)
- Bouncy, energetic posing — never still
- Slightly oversized gear exaggerates his smallness

## The Emotional Stakes

Max is where the movie's emotional power lives.

**The moment that makes parents cry:**

Max sits on the bench during the championship game. Rob isn't there — he's saving the city. Other dads cheer. Max watches quietly. Then says:

> "It's okay… my dad saves bigger things."

And later, when Rob asks:

> "Do you still think I'm a superhero?"

Max says:

> "No."

Pause.

> "You're better. You're my dad."

## Hockey as Connection

Hockey is the thread between Max and Rob's Canadian identity. In their city, hockey isn't the main sport — it's their thing. Rob brought it from Canada. Max inherited the love. The rink is where father and son connect, and Max's championship game is the emotional anchor of the climax.

## The Silence

This is the most devastating structural event in the film.

### What Happens

Mid-Act Two (~40 minutes in), after Rob misses bedtime one too many times, Max quietly stops narrating. He doesn't announce it. He doesn't say "I don't believe in you anymore." He just... stops.

- The dramatic whispers disappear
- The action figures go in a drawer
- The dish-towel cape stays folded on his chair
- He stops drawing Rocket Rob

### What It Means

Max isn't angry. He isn't resentful. He's outgrowing the fantasy. This isn't a tantrum — it's growing up. And growing up is worse, because you can't fix it with an apology.

But The Silence isn't just emotional. It's structural. When Max stops believing, the film doesn't just lose its fun — it loses its **power source**. Rob becomes measurably less effective in the scenes that follow. He hesitates where he didn't before. Makes mistakes. Loses confidence. He's slower, less certain, less brave. The belief that was making him better went quiet, and the audience should feel that something has been taken from him beyond just the narration.

### What the Audience Feels

The film loses its magic track. Scenes that would have been narrated by Max — Rob driving to work, Rob at the office, Rob doing mundane things — play out in plain, quiet reality. No superhero overlay. No imagination. Just... life.

Kids feel something is wrong. They can't name it, but the fun part of the movie went away.

Parents feel grief. They recognize this. It's the moment your kid stops playing pretend. It's the moment the magic ends.

### The Silence in Practice

| Before the Silence | After the Silence |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Rob fixes something → Max narrates it as a mission | Rob fixes something → just a man fixing something |
| Rob drives to work → "Rocket launch!" | Rob drives to work → traffic |
| Rob comes home late → "Secret mission complete!" | Rob comes home late → Max is already asleep |

The contrast should feel like color draining from the world. Not literally (the palette stays warm) but emotionally. The voice that gave this movie its soul goes quiet.

### The Return

At the very end of the film, after the battle, after the hug, after "Just the parts that matter" — Max's voice comes back one last time. The pull-back. The city. The stars.

> "Every city has a hero. Ours has the best one."

But it's different. Softer. Sleepier. More certain. This isn't a kid playing pretend. This is a child who outgrew the fantasy, sat in the silence, and **chose** to believe anyway.

This is the power coming back on. But different. Stronger. Because naive faith is free — it costs nothing to believe when you've never been let down. A *choice* to believe, after the silence, after the missed games and the broken promises and the growing up — that costs something. And because it costs something, it's more powerful than it ever was before.

That's the Pixar moment. That's the breakthrough. The superpower was never Rob's. It was always Max's. And the most heroic thing anyone does in this film is an eight-year-old choosing to believe in an imperfect dad.

## Character Constellation Role

**The Power Source — tested by silence.** Max sits at the base of the constellation because he is the power source. Belief flows *up*. Rob's heroism is powered by Max's imagination. The constellation's energy starts at the bottom.

Max's belief is: "My dad is already a hero." But unlike a static foundation, Max's belief is *tested* by the silence. He represents childhood faith — the kind that doesn't need proof, just presence. When that presence disappears, the faith goes quiet and the power goes with it. When it returns, it's no longer faith. It's a choice. And a choice is more powerful than faith, because it was earned.
