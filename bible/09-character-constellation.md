# Rocket Rob — Character Constellation

In story development at studios like Pixar, the Character Constellation maps how every major character represents a different belief about the film's theme — and how each character *changes* across the story.

## The Theme

> "Being a hero isn't about saving the world. It's about showing up for the people who believe in you."

**The underneath truth**: Rob didn't become a hero and then earn Max's belief. Max's belief is what made Rob a hero. Belief is the superpower — and it was always Max's.

Every character either supports, challenges, or distorts this belief. When they clash, the story naturally becomes emotional and compelling.

## The Constellation

```
                    Victor Watt

               (The False Answer)

      Shadow Dad                    Coach Hank
   "Work First"                  "Show Up First"
  (Rob's Fear)                  (Tough Love Truth)

                    ROCKET ROB
              "Trying to balance"
                (The Question)

       Claire                    [Cat Sidekick]
  "Protect what               "Just show up"
     matters"                (Loyalty Without
  (Was a hero first;             Asking)
   carries it alone)

                      Max

           (The Power Source

```

Max sits beneath Rob because he is the power source. Belief flows *up*. Rob's heroism is powered by Max's imagination. The constellation's energy starts at the bottom.
Watt sits above as the ideological challenge.
Claire sits beside Rob — equal, parallel, carrying her own arc.
Everything pushes Rob toward his final realization — but the power to reach it comes from below.

## Character Arcs

### Rocket Rob — The Center

**Belief at start**: "I want to be a great dad… but I'm afraid I'm failing."

**Belief at end**: "The people who love you are the reason to be heroic."

**Arc**: Rob lives in the tension between responsibility, ambition, family, and expectations. He begins believing maybe real heroes sacrifice their families. Through the cost of the double life, Max's silence, Claire's reveal, and the drawing in his belt buckle, he realizes the opposite.

### Max — The Power Source (Tested by Silence)

**Belief at start**: "My dad is already a hero."

**The underneath truth**: Max's belief isn't a reaction to Rob's heroism. It's the *cause* of it. Every whisper of "Rocket Rob saves the day" was giving Rob the courage to try. Belief flows up through the constellation. Max is the power source.

**The Silence**: Mid-film, Max outgrows the Rocket Rob fantasy. He stops narrating. Stops drawing. The power goes offline. This isn't anger — it's growing up. The movie loses its magic track, and Rob loses his power source. He becomes less effective, less brave, less present. The audience feels the hero diminish.

**Belief at end**: Same words. Different weight. Max's final narration — "Every city has a hero. Ours has the best one." — is not a kid playing pretend. It's a child who sat in the silence and chose to believe anyway. The power comes back on — but different, because a choice to believe is more powerful than naive faith. It costs something. And that cost makes it real. This is the most heroic act in the film.

### Claire (Vela) — The Grounding Force (With Her Own Arc)

**Belief**: "Great heroes protect what matters most."

**Flaw**: She believes she has to protect it alone. She was Vela. She chose family. She carries that choice — and the invisible load of everything Rob doesn't see — in silence.

**Arc**: From carrying everything alone to letting Rob (and the audience) see her. The Vela reveal at the second-act turning point recontextualizes the entire film. Her B-theme — "Being strong doesn't mean doing it alone" — resolves when she and Rob fight as a team in Act Three, and stand together in the kitchen at the end.

### Victor Watt — The Opposite Philosophy

**Belief**: "Greatness requires sacrifice — especially family."

**Function**: Watt is Rob's dark mirror. Where Rob chose family, Watt chose power. He represents obsession, ego, and loneliness. He is the "False Answer" to the theme.

**Exit**: Not defeated by force. Defeated by being proven wrong. "You were always the better one of us." The photo he carries tells the audience he could have been Rob.

### Coach Hank — Tough Love Mentor

**Belief**: "Showing up matters more than winning."

**Function**: Reinforces the theme in a grounded, real-world way. No superpowers needed. His wisdom lands because it's simple and earned.

### [Cat Sidekick] — Comic Relief with Loyalty

**Belief**: "Showing up is enough."

**Function**: Unlike a character who needs to *learn* love, the cat already has it — he just expresses it sideways. He takes everything Rob says as direct instruction, appearing in situations that require him to be there and disappearing when nothing heroic is happening. He doesn't solve problems with logic or heart. He solves them by being present when nobody asked him to be. That's the theme in its purest, furriest form.

**Name**: Maple.

### The Shadow Dad — Rob's Fear

**Belief**: "Work first. Family later."

**Function**: A brief psychological antagonist in Act Two. The version of Rob if he stops trying. Powerful. Successful. Empty. "They'll understand when they're older." Rob realizes: *They won't.*

## How the Constellation Creates Story

Every major scene involves at least two constellation positions in tension:

| Scene | Characters in Tension | Theme Argument |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| Opening (kitchen) | Rob + Max | Max sees a hero; Rob sees a tired dad |
| Establishing world | Rob + Claire | Claire carries what Rob doesn't see |
| Watt's announcement | Rob + Watt | Family vs. ambition |
| The double life | Rob + Claire + Max + Cat | Each save costs family; Claire absorbs the cost; cat keeps showing up |
| The Silence begins | Max (alone) | Faith tested by absence |
| Hockey game | Rob + Max + Coach Hank | Showing up vs. saving the world |
| Shadow Dad moment | Rob + Shadow Dad | Who Rob fears becoming |
| Claire confronts Rob | Rob + Claire | "I know what you're trying to do. I've done it." |
| Vela reveal | Claire (center) | Recontextualizes everything |
| Final battle | Rob + Claire + Watt | Two arcs converge; both heroes, together |
| The resolution | Rob + Max + Claire | Theme resolved through love; narration returns |

## Why Pixar Uses This System

This structure makes films like Inside Out, The Incredibles, and Coco feel emotionally layered. Every character is secretly arguing about the same idea. Kids enjoy the action and humor. Adults feel the emotional debate about family and life.

Rocket Rob adds a second layer Pixar hasn't done: **two parallel arcs** (Rob's A-arc and Claire's B-arc) that collide at the same turning point and resolve in the same quiet kitchen scene. The dual devastation — a dad learning to show up, a mom learning to let go of carrying it alone — hits parents from two directions at once.
