# Rocket Rob — Series Expansion

> The following episode concepts expand the Rocket Rob world beyond the feature film. The movie establishes the characters, theme, and emotional core. The companion series deepens the world, introduces new villains, and explores the family's life after the events of the film — with Max's narration restored, Claire operating openly as Velocity, and Rob balancing heroism with fatherhood in a world that now knows who he is.

## Series Format

Each episode combines a real-world family scenario with a superhero adventure that mirrors or amplifies the emotional stakes. The mundane becomes the epic through Max's imagination — which, after the film's events, carries a different weight.

**Structure**: Every episode has two parallel tracks:
1. **The Real Problem** — A relatable family/kid challenge
2. **The Hero Problem** — A villain or crisis that metaphorically mirrors the real problem

The audience gradually stops being sure which track is "real."

## Season 1 Episodes

### Episode 1: "The Greatest Hero Ever"

**The Real Problem**: Rob is late to pick up Max from hockey practice — again. He promises to make it to the championship game.

**The Hero Problem**: Victor Volt announces his energy technology. When Rob pushes back on its safety, Volt begins his descent into villainy.

**Theme**: Setting the stakes — can Rob be the dad Max believes in?

*This episode contains the opening sequence and establishes the world, tone, and central conflict.*

---

### Episode 2: "The Missing Hockey Stick"

**The Real Problem**: Max's lucky hockey stick disappears before a big game. Max is convinced he can't play without it.

**The Hero Problem**: Rocket Rob must track down the stick through increasingly absurd obstacles — the school lost-and-found is a labyrinth, the neighbor's garage is a villain's lair.

**Theme**: Confidence comes from inside, not from lucky charms. Rob teaches Max that the magic was always in him.

---

### Episode 3: "Laundry Day Apocalypse"

**The Real Problem**: Claire is away for work. Rob is in charge of the house. Everything spirals.

**The Hero Problem**: The washing machine becomes a portal. Socks become sentient. The dryer is a black hole. Rob must restore order before Claire gets home.

**Theme**: Appreciating what your partner does. Rob gains new respect for Claire's invisible work.

---

### Episode 4: "The Science Fair Disaster"

**The Real Problem**: Max's science fair project (a volcano, obviously) won't work. The fair is tomorrow.

**The Hero Problem**: The Homework Hydra attacks — every solution creates two new problems. Rob and Max must work together, combining dad logic with kid imagination.

**Theme**: The process matters more than the result. Max learns that trying together is better than winning alone.

---

### Episode 5: "The Homework Monster"

**The Real Problem**: Max has a huge assignment due. He can't focus. Rob can't help without doing it for him.

**The Hero Problem**: The Homework Hydra returns — each head (math, science, spelling, history) must be defeated differently. The cat sidekick keeps showing up and accidentally solving the wrong problem at exactly the right moment.

**Theme**: Learning to struggle. Rob must let Max fail a little to help him grow.

---

### Episode 6: "Dad vs. Bedtime"

**The Real Problem**: Max refuses to go to sleep. Every excuse in the book. One more story. One more drink of water. One more question about the universe.

**The Hero Problem**: Each delay opens a new mini-adventure. The glass of water becomes a quest to the kitchen (lava floor). The bedtime story becomes a live mission. The "one more question" leads to a philosophical confrontation with the Shadow Dad.

**Theme**: Even bedtime is a kind of heroism. Presence matters more than perfection.

---

### Episode 7: "Coach Crusher"

**The Real Problem**: Max's team faces Coach Crusher's hyper-competitive squad. Crusher's kids are drilled, serious, and scared. Max's team plays for fun.

**The Hero Problem**: Crusher's philosophy — "winning is everything" — starts to infect Max. He becomes aggressive on the ice. Rob must intervene without becoming the overbearing sports dad.

**Theme**: What are we teaching kids when we teach them to win? Coach Hank's wisdom shines here.

---

### Episode 8: "The Deadline"

**The Real Problem**: Rob has an impossible work deadline at Titan Sports. He's been pulling late nights. Max notices. Claire worries.

**The Hero Problem**: The Deadline (villain) literally manifests from Rob's work stress — emails, notifications, buzzing phones forming a chaos monster that prevents Rob from leaving the office.

**Theme**: Work will always expand to fill the time you give it. The only way to beat The Deadline is to choose to leave.

---

### Episode 9: "Little Hero Moments"

**The Real Problem**: Max finds old home videos and photos. He sees baby-Max with Rob. He starts asking about when he was little. Rob realizes how fast time is moving.

**The Hero Problem**: Minimal. This is the quiet episode. The Up-style montage lives here — "Little Hero Moments" plays over a sequence of memories.

**Theme**: Time is the real villain. And the only way to fight it is to be present right now.

*This is the episode that makes parents cry.*

---

### Episode 10: "What Makes a Hero" (Season Finale)

**The Real Problem**: Max's championship hockey game is today. Rob promises to be there. Victor Volt launches his full attack on the city.

**The Hero Problem**: Volt's power machine threatens the entire city grid. Rob must stop him. But the game is in two hours. Then one. Then it's started.

**Theme**: "Being a hero isn't about saving the world. It's about showing up for the people who believe in you."

*This episode contains the final battle, the hockey game scene, and the emotional resolution.*

## Episode Structure Template

Each episode follows this approximate structure:

| Beat | Duration | Content |
|------|----------|---------|
| Cold Open | 2 min | Max narration over a mundane-turned-epic moment |
| Act 1 | 5 min | The real-world problem is established |
| Act 2A | 7 min | The hero problem mirrors the real problem; Rob tries to solve both |
| Act 2B | 5 min | Things get worse; the two tracks converge |
| Act 3 | 4 min | Resolution — emotional truth lands through action |
| Tag | 2 min | Quiet family moment; Max's narration bookends |

**Total runtime**: ~25 minutes per episode

## Series Arc

Across the season, the episodes build toward:

1. Rob becoming more comfortable as Rocket Rob
2. Max slowly beginning to see the line between imagination and reality
3. Victor Volt escalating from philosophical rival to genuine threat
4. The question of whether Rocket Rob is real growing more ambiguous
5. Claire's hidden depth becoming harder to ignore
6. The championship game as the emotional finish line
