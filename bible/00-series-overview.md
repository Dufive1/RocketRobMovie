# Rocket Rob — Movie Overview

## Logline

A well-meaning Canadian dad discovers that the superhero his eight-year-old son imagined him to be might be more real than either of them expected — because it turns out a child's belief isn't just a reaction to who you are. It's the thing that makes you who you become.

## Format

Pixar animated feature film. Runtime: ~90 minutes.

## Core Theme (A-Arc)

**"Can an ordinary dad still be a hero?"**

Kids see a superhero. Parents see a dad trying his best. Rocket Rob is born from the space between imagination and reality — a story about what it means to be someone's hero when you're just doing your best.

### The Underneath Truth

Rob didn't become a hero and then earn Max's belief. **Max's belief is what made Rob a hero.** Every time Max whispered "Rocket Rob saves the day," he was doing something real — giving his dad the courage to try. Belief is the superpower, and it was always Max's.

When that belief goes silent mid-film, the power goes with it. Rob becomes less brave, less capable, less present. The audience feels the hero diminish before they understand why. And when Max's voice returns at the end — not as faith, but as a choice — the power is different. Stronger. Because a choice to believe costs something, and that cost makes it real.

## B-Theme (Claire's Arc)

**"Being strong doesn't mean doing it alone."**

Claire Lavoie has been carrying the family — and a secret hero identity — alone for years. Her arc runs parallel to Rob's: while he learns that showing up is heroism, she learns that letting others in makes you stronger. These two arcs intersect at the Vela reveal, the film's second-act turning point.

## Sub-Themes

- **Kids growing up** — Max outgrows the Rocket Rob fantasy mid-film. His narration goes silent. That silence is the most devastating thing in the movie.
- **Parents trying their best** — The constant tension of never feeling like you're doing enough
- **Work vs. family** — Titan Sports Tech demands Rob's time; his family demands his heart
- **Imagination vs. real life** — Where does Max's version of Rocket Rob end and the real Rob begin?
- **Belief as power** — Max's imagination doesn't just color the story, it powers it. When it stops, everything dims. The film's core philosophical commitment.
- **The invisible load** — Claire holds everything together so seamlessly that nobody notices until it almost breaks her

## Target Audience

| Audience | Age | Hook |
|----------|-----|------|
| Primary | Kids 6–12 | Superhero action, humor, imaginative world, hockey |
| Secondary | Parents | Emotional family story, parenting themes, the quiet sacrifices |
| Tertiary | General animation fans | Pixar-quality storytelling, character depth, visual polish |

The dual-audience structure is the engine: every scene works on two levels. Max narrating Rob fixing the sink as "repairing the Hydro Blaster" is comedy for kids and a warm parenting moment for adults. When that narration disappears mid-film, kids feel something is wrong. Parents feel grief.

## Tone

- **Pixar-level emotional storytelling** — Humor on the surface, real feeling underneath
- **Optimistic but honest** — Rob is tired, overwhelmed, and imperfect. But he keeps showing up.
- **Playful and imaginative** — Max's narration transforms the mundane into the epic
- **Never cynical** — Even the villains have relatable pain. This world believes in people.

## What Makes Rocket Rob Different

Every superhero film gives the hero powers and asks "can they handle it?" Rocket Rob gives a *child* the power — belief — and asks "what happens when it goes away?"

The Incredibles starts with superheroes and finds the family story. Rocket Rob starts with a family and finds the superhero story. The twist that Rocket Rob might only exist in Max's imagination gives the film an emotional dimension that superhero properties don't typically reach — because if the superpower is belief, then the eight-year-old is the most powerful character in the movie.

The second twist — that Claire was a hero first, and chose family over the cape — gives parents a second emotional devastation they didn't see coming.

Rob isn't trying to save the world. He's trying to make it home for bedtime. And the only reason he has the courage to do either is because a kid in a hockey helmet thinks he can.
