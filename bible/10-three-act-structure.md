# Rocket Rob — Three-Act Structure

## Format

Pixar animated feature film. Runtime: ~90 minutes.

## Dual-Arc Engine

The film runs two parallel character arcs that intersect at the climax:

- **Rob's A-Arc**: "Can an ordinary dad still be a hero?" He learns that showing up IS heroism.
- **Claire's B-Arc**: "Being strong doesn't mean doing it alone." She learns that letting others in makes you stronger.

These arcs collide at the Vela reveal — the moment the audience realizes Claire was a hero first, and chose family over the cape.

## The Film's Philosophical Commitment

**Belief is the superpower.** Max's imagination doesn't just color the story — it powers Rob's heroism. Every whisper of "Rocket Rob saves the day" was doing something real: giving an ordinary dad the courage to try. When the belief goes silent mid-film, the hero diminishes. When it returns at the end — not as faith, but as a *choice* — it's more powerful than it ever was. The most heroic act in this film isn't Rob absorbing the machine or Claire revealing she's Vela. It's an eight-year-old choosing to believe in an imperfect dad.

## Story-Level Events

Two structural events shape the film beyond plot:

1. **Max's narration disappears** (mid-Act Two). The superhero voice that colors the entire film goes silent. The imagination track stops. The audience feels something is wrong before they can name it. This is the emotional equivalent of the color draining from the world in Inside Out. But it's more than tonal — the *power source goes offline*. Rob should become measurably less effective in the second half of Act Two. He hesitates. He makes mistakes. The audience should feel that something has been taken from him beyond just the fun of the narration. The belief that made him brave is gone.

2. **Max's narration returns** (final moment). Soft, sleepy, certain. But different. It's not a kid playing pretend anymore. It's a child who chose to believe. The power comes back on — stronger, because a choice costs something that faith doesn't.

## ACT ONE: "The Greatest Hero Ever" (~25 minutes)

### Opening (~0:00–5:00)

**Sequence**: "The Greatest Hero Ever" (see `scripts/opening-sequence.md`)

Black screen. Max's voice, hushed and dramatic: "Every city has a hero... but OUR city has the greatest hero EVER."

Epic superhero battle: Rocket Rob vs. Victor Watt over the city skyline. Action, lightning, saves, Max narrating the whole thing.

SMASH CUT to Rob on his back under the kitchen sink. Water everywhere. Max in the doorway in pajamas and a hockey helmet, whispering: "Rocket Rob repairs the Hydro Blaster."

Claire walks in. "Did the city survive?" Rob: "Barely."

Rob alone: "Greatest hero ever." He doesn't quite believe it.

TITLE CARD: ROCKET ROB

### Establishing the World (~5:00–12:00)

Rob's morning routine — getting Max to school, driving to Titan Sports Tech, navigating his cubicle (Team Canada mug, Max's drawings pinned to the wall, family photos).

Max at school and hockey practice. Coach Hank introduced. Max narrates everything — Rob dropping him off is a "rocket launch," the school hallway is a "mission corridor."

Claire at home and work — capable, organized, subtly more competent than she lets on. Quick, knowing reactions when Rob describes something "weird" that happened. She moves a little too fast. Catches things she shouldn't be able to catch. The audience doesn't notice yet. But it's there.

The cat sidekick introduced — an orange tabby who has claimed Rob as his human and wears a repurposed Titan Sports Tech harness (with gadgets he probably knocked off Rob's desk). Comic relief. Takes everything Rob says as direct instruction.

Max's imagination track is in full force: when Rob is late from work, Max narrates why — and the audience sees his invented villains, Dr. Overtime and The Countdown, as the enemies keeping Rocket Rob from coming home. These imagination villains appear throughout Acts One and early Two as part of the colorful overlay. When The Silence falls, they disappear along with everything else Max created.

### The Montage (~12:00–18:00)

**Sequence**: "Little Hero Moments" (see `scripts/little-hero-moments.md`)

Silent. Piano and guitar. No dialogue.

The montage bonds the audience to this family: tiny Max in a dish-towel cape, first time on skates, Rob reading bedtime stories with dramatic voices, late nights at Titan, Max's drawings accumulating on the fridge, time passing.

Final shot: Max hands Rob a drawing — Rocket Rob flying with a hockey stick. "Every hero needs a sidekick." Claire watches, and her eyes say what her mouth doesn't: *someday Max will grow up.*

### Inciting Incident (~18:00–25:00)

Victor Watt reappears. Former Titan colleague, now powerful and solo. Unveils his "revolutionary energy technology" at a public event. Rob attends. Something feels wrong about it.

Rob confronts Volt privately. Volt's philosophy surfaces: "Family makes you weak." Rob refuses to support the project.

Volt activates the machine. The city grid flickers. Power surges. This isn't innovation — it's control.

**Act One Turning Point**: The city is in danger. Rob must decide — be the hero Max imagines, or stay safe. He chooses to act.

## ACT TWO: "The Cost" (~45 minutes)

### Rising Action — The Double Life (~25:00–40:00)

Rob tries to be Rocket Rob AND Rob Lavoie. Every save costs family time.

- He stops a power surge downtown. Misses hockey practice pickup. Claire covers.
- He dismantles one of Volt's relay stations. Gets home late. Max is already asleep.
- He intercepts a grid failure at the hospital. Misses Max's pre-game skate.

Each time, Max's narration covers for him: "Rocket Rob is on a secret mission!" But the narration gets quieter. Less excited. More routine.

Claire is holding everything together. She doesn't complain — she just does more. Drives Max. Handles the house. Covers for Rob's absences. But the invisible load is becoming visible. She's tired. She's sharp where she used to be warm.

The cat keeps showing up in Rob's hero moments — underfoot at the worst times, accidentally effective at the right ones. ("You said 'let's wing it'." *Cat deploys off the nearest surface.*)

### The Silence Begins (~40:00–50:00)

Rob misses bedtime. Again. Max is awake when he gets home.

Max doesn't narrate. He just looks at Rob.

                              MAX
                    You missed the story.

                              ROB
                    I know. I'm sorry, bud. Tomorrow—

                              MAX
                    You said that yesterday.

Max rolls over. Goes to sleep.

No narration. No "Rocket Rob returns from his mission." Just a kid who's tired of waiting.

Over the next several scenes, the narration fades completely. Max stops whispering hero commentary. He puts the action figures in a drawer. The dish-towel cape stays folded on his chair.

The movie's magic track goes silent. Scenes that would have been narrated — Rob at work, Rob in the car, Rob doing mundane things — play out in plain, quiet reality. The audience feels the absence before they can name it.

And Rob changes. Without the belief powering him, he's slower, less certain, less brave. He hesitates before acting. Makes tactical mistakes he wouldn't have made in Act One. His confidence erodes. The power source is offline, and the hero is diminishing. The audience should feel this as a physical thing — something has been taken from Rob, not just from the soundtrack.

**The Hockey Game** (~50:00–58:00)

**Sequence**: "The Hockey Game" (see `scripts/hockey-game-scene.md`)

Championship day. Rob promises to be there. Volt's machine escalates — citywide blackouts.

Rob saves the city. Misses the game.

Max sits on the bench. Watches other dads cheer. Claire holds the empty seat beside her.

Max, quietly: "It's okay... my dad saves bigger things."

Rob arrives late. No cape. Wrinkled shirt. Bruised. He ran.

Max sees him. The two-tap signal. Max plays harder. Game ends. Max hugs him. No words. Just the hug.

### The Low Point (~58:00–65:00)

Rob is exhausted. Overwhelmed. Failing at both lives.

**The Shadow Dad moment**: A brief, internal sequence. Rob sees himself — or a version of himself — polished, successful, empty. The Shadow Dad doesn't attack. He just stands there and says:

> "They'll understand when they're older."

Rob realizes: *They won't.*

Volt's words start echoing: "You can save the world or be there for your family. Not both." Rob starts to believe it.

Claire confronts Rob. Not with anger — with honesty:

                              CLAIRE
                    You're trying to save everyone
                    except the people in this house.

                              ROB
                    I'm trying to—

                              CLAIRE
                    I know what you're trying to do.
                    I've done it.

Beat. Rob doesn't understand. Yet.

### The Vela Reveal (~65:00–70:00)

Volt launches a targeted attack on the Lavoie neighborhood. Power fails. The house goes dark.

Claire moves. Not like a worried mom. Like someone who's done this before.

She gets Max safe. She reroutes the house power with a move Rob doesn't recognize. She positions herself between the surge and Max with a speed that isn't normal.

Rob sees it. The knowing looks. The calm in crisis. The way she always seemed to understand things she shouldn't.

                              ROB
                    Claire... how did you—

Claire looks at him. The mask drops. Not a superhero mask. The other kind — the one she's been wearing for years.

                              CLAIRE
                    I was Vela before you were
                    Rocket Rob.

The audience recontextualizes everything. Her calm. Her competence. Her knowing looks when Rob described his powers. She wasn't learning — she was remembering.

                              ROB
                    Why did you stop?

                              CLAIRE
                         (looking toward Max's room)
                    Because I chose something harder.

This is Claire's B-arc thesis: she chose family over the cape. And it was harder. And it was worth it. But she carried the weight of that choice alone — and that's what she needs to stop doing.

**Act Two Turning Point**: Rob understands two things. Claire sacrificed her hero identity for the family. And he's about to sacrifice the family for his hero identity. He's becoming what she walked away from.

---

## ACT THREE: "What Makes a Hero" (~20 minutes)

### The Final Battle (~70:00–82:00)

**Sequence**: "What Makes a Hero" (see `scripts/final-battle.md`)

Massive storm. Volt's machine at full power atop Titan tower.

But this time, Rob isn't alone. Claire is Vela again — not because she wants to be, but because the family needs her to be.

She coordinates the ground: evacuating civilians, protecting infrastructure, shielding the arena where Max's rescheduled game is happening. Rob takes the sky: heading for Volt's machine.

They work as a team. For the first time, Claire lets Rob lead the hero mission while she handles what she's always handled — keeping the family safe. But now she's doing it openly, not invisibly.

On the rooftop, Rob confronts Volt. The philosophy clash. "Heroes don't tuck kids into bed."

Rob finds Max's drawing in his belt buckle. "My Dad Saves The World."

He understands. Max never believed because of the city. Max believed because Rob showed up.

Rob absorbs the machine's energy. Stabilizes the grid. No explosion. No dramatic moment. Just the right choice.

Volt, broken:

> "...Why?"

Rob:

> "Because someone's waiting for me at home."

**Volt's Exit**: "You were always the better one of us." He pauses. Reaches into his coat. Pulls out something small — a creased, old photo he's carried for years. We don't see what's in it. He looks at it. Puts it back. Steps off the edge and disappears.

The audience understands: he could have been Rob.

### The Resolution (~82:00–88:00)

**The Arena**: Rob arrives. Late. Again. But he's there. Bruised. Bandaged. Smiling.

Max sees him. The two taps. The hug. "Did you win?" Max shrugs. Doesn't matter. He showed up.

**Max's Bedroom**: Night. Rob sits on the edge of the bed. Max stirs.

> "Did you save the world?"
> "Something like that."
> "I knew it."

**The Kitchen**: Rob at the fridge. Claire in the doorway.

> "Still saving the world?"
> "Just the parts that matter."

They stand together. Quiet. Max's drawings all around them.

### The Final Image (~88:00–90:00)

Pull back through the kitchen window. The house. The street. The hockey net. The neighborhood. The city. The skyline. Stars.

And then — Max's voice. One last time.

Not the dramatic, excited whisper from the opening. Something softer. Sleepier. More certain.

                              MAX (V.O.)
                    Every city has a hero.
                    Ours has the best one.

But it's different now. This isn't a kid playing pretend. This is an eight-year-old who outgrew the fantasy, sat in the silence, and chose to believe anyway.

This is the power coming back on. But different. Chosen. And a chosen belief is more powerful than a given one — because it costs something. The most heroic act in the film isn't Rob's sacrifice on the rooftop. It's Max's choice in this final moment.

That's the Pixar moment.

TITLE CARD: **ROCKET ROB**

FADE TO BLACK.
