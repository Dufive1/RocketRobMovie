# Rocket Rob — Story Spine

The Pixar Story Spine is a tool used at Pixar Animation Studios to test whether a story has emotional power. It reduces the entire film to one emotional chain of events.

## Theme Stated

> "Being a hero isn't about saving the world. It's about being there for the people who believe in you."

Kids hear a superhero story. Parents hear something deeper.

### The Underneath Truth

Rob didn't become a hero and then earn Max's belief. Max's belief is what made Rob a hero. Belief is the superpower — and it was always Max's.

## B-Theme (Claire)

> "Being strong doesn't mean doing it alone."

---

## Once upon a time…

Rob Lavoie was an ordinary dad who worked a demanding job at Titan Sports Tech, rushing between meetings, emails, and deadlines while trying his best to be present for his son Max.

But in Max's eyes, Rob wasn't ordinary.

He was **Rocket Rob** — the greatest superhero in the world.

And Claire — his wife, his partner, the calm center of their family — held everything together so seamlessly that nobody noticed. Not even Rob. Because Claire had a secret: she used to be a hero too.

## Every day…

Rob tried to balance work, family, and life.

Max turned everything Rob did into a superhero adventure:

- Fixing the sink became stopping a flood monster
- Driving to hockey practice became a rocket launch
- Bedtime stories became missions to save the universe

Rob laughed along, but deep down he worried he was failing at both work and fatherhood.

Claire carried the invisible load — the pickups, the dinners, the bedtimes, the emotional architecture of their family — and never complained. She watched Rob and Max play superheroes, and sometimes her eyes said something her mouth didn't.

## One day…

Rob's former colleague Victor Watt unveils a revolutionary energy technology that could power the entire city.

But Watt has a darker plan. He believes the world needs great heroes who sacrifice everything — including family — to change it.

When Rob refuses to help him, Watt turns his invention into a dangerous power machine that threatens the city.

Suddenly Rob must decide:

Be the hero Max believes in…

Or stay the safe, ordinary dad he thinks he is.

## Because of that…

Rob begins secretly trying to stop Watt while still showing up for Max.

But every heroic moment comes at a cost. He misses:

- Hockey practice
- Bedtime stories
- The championship game

Max keeps defending him at first. But something shifts. The dramatic whispers get quieter. The narration that colored every scene starts to fade. Max isn't angry — he's outgrowing the fantasy.

And then the narration stops entirely. The action figures go in a drawer. The cape stays folded on his chair. The movie's magic goes silent. And Rob — without the belief powering him — becomes less effective. Slower. Less brave. He hesitates where he didn't before. The power source is dimming.

Meanwhile, Claire absorbs everything Rob can't do. She drives Max. She handles the house. She carries the weight. She's tired. Sharp where she used to be warm. The invisible load is cracking.

## Because of that…

Rob pushes himself harder trying to be both hero and dad.

He becomes exhausted, overwhelmed, and unsure if he's doing the right thing. Watt's philosophy — "You can save the world or be there for your family. Not both" — starts to feel true.

In his lowest moment, Rob encounters the Shadow Dad — a vision of himself if he chose work over family. Successful. Powerful. Empty. "They'll understand when they're older." Rob realizes: *They won't.*

Claire confronts Rob — not with anger, but with honesty: "You're trying to save everyone except the people in this house." And then, quietly: "I know what you're trying to do. I've done it."

## Until finally…

Watt attacks the Lavoie neighborhood. The house goes dark. Claire moves like someone who's done this before. Gets Max safe. Reroutes power. Shields her family with speed that isn't normal.

Rob sees it. Claire drops the mask — not a superhero mask, but the one she's been wearing for years.

"I was Vela before you were Rocket Rob."

Rob understands two things: Claire sacrificed her hero identity for the family. And he's about to sacrifice the family for his. He's becoming what she walked away from.

Together — for the first time as partners — they face Watt. Claire coordinates the ground. Rob takes the sky. She lets him lead the mission. He lets her be seen. They're a team.

Rob confronts Watt. Finds Max's drawing in his belt buckle. "My Dad Saves The World." He understands: Max believed because Rob showed up, not because he saved the city.

Rob absorbs the machine's energy. Stabilizes the grid. No dramatic explosion. Just the right choice.

Watt, broken, reaches for a photo he's carried for years. "You were always the better one of us." He steps off the roof and disappears.

## And ever since then…

Rob still has a normal job. Still rushes between meetings. Still sometimes arrives late.

But Claire doesn't carry the weight alone anymore. And Rob sees her — really sees her — for the first time.

And Max? Max's voice comes back one last time. Soft. Sleepy. Certain.

> "Every city has a hero. Ours has the best one."

But it's different now. This isn't a kid playing pretend. This is a child who outgrew the fantasy, sat in the silence, and chose to believe anyway.

## The Emotional Engine

| Layer | What the Audience Sees |
|-------|----------------------|
| Surface | A superhero fights a villain and saves the city |
| Middle | A dad tries to balance work, family, and an impossible situation |
| Deep | A mom has been carrying a secret and a family alone for years |
| Core | A child's belief is the superpower. It made Rob a hero. Its absence broke him. Its return — as a choice, not faith — is the most powerful moment in the film. |

## Why This Works

This story spine combines the emotional DNA of:

- **The Incredibles** — Family hero story, secret identity, work-life tension
- **Inside Out** — Emotional growth, the thing you didn't know you needed
- **Toy Story** — Being someone's hero, the fear of not being enough
- **Up** — The quiet devastation, the love story underneath

But with three unique twists:

1. **The everyday dad as the superhero.** Not a retired hero rediscovering powers — just a guy whose kid believes in him so much it might actually be real.
2. **The mom who was a hero first and chose family.** The second emotional devastation parents didn't see coming.
3. **The superpower isn't a mutation or a suit. It's an eight-year-old's belief.** And when it goes silent, you feel the power leave the room. When it returns — not as faith, but as a choice — you feel it come back stronger. That's the breakthrough.
